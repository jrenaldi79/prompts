# DIGGING DEEPER: THE LADDERING INTERVIEW, A TOOL FOR SURFACING VALUES

# Philip J. Trocchia

# University of South Florida–St. Petersburg

# Diane L. Swanson

# Kansas State University

# Marc Orlitzky

# University of Redlands

Personally held values play a fundamental role in business. As such, it is critical that students understand the nature of values pertaining to the workplace. Using an innovative classroom exercise, laddering, business students interview individuals to identify values that influence choices. Objectives are to help students understand the role of personal values in decision making, develop the ability to respond effectively to workplace demands, respect diverse perspectives, and achieve a better understanding of their own values. Measures used to assess these learning objectives revealed that many students gained valuable insights. After participating in the exercise, students wrote passages in which they articulated how their values affected their decision-making behavior, described in detail how the exercise improved their improvisational skills, and helped them gain respect for others’ viewpoints. Furthermore, a quantitative survey provided evidence that students may have experienced a greater understanding of their own values after participating in the exercise.

Keywords: laddering; management education; values; classroom learning exercise; means–end

Values help shape environments external to business in that they drive consumer preferences for goods and services (Burgess, 1992), influence stakeholders’ expectations (Frederick & Weber, 1987), and shape public policy (Etzioni, 1988). Values may also be important to consider as important factors influencing employee motivation (Locke & Latham, 2004), communication, and stakeholder analysis (Agle, Mitchell, & Sonnenfeld, 1999).

Furthermore, the recent eruption of corporate malfeasance in such highly publicized cases as Ford-Firestone, Global Crossings, Enron, WorldCom, and Tyco underscores the need to understand the values relevant to business. As normative factors in decision making, values are powerful determinants of corporate cultures, either actively or by default (Schein, 1992).

However, in all these (and many other) areas, research into values is relatively new and, thus, could be expanded in meaningful ways. It is with these issues in mind that we crafted an exercise to expose students to the practical role of values in decision making. We did so because, although values are germane to understanding such topics as ethical issues in domestic and global business, organizational cultures, consumer behavior, and government regulation of industries and markets, normative analysis is sparse in business school curricula (Gioia, 2002; Kelly, 2002; Swanson & Frederick, 2005; Waddock, 2003). Indeed, only one third of Association to Advance Collegiate Schools of Business (AACSB)-accredited business schools even offer a business ethics course (Willen, 2004), a logical vehicle for such analysis. As a result, thousands of students graduate each year without a practical knowledge of the role values play in their chosen vocations (Windsor, 2002). According to two studies, this myopia gets worse as students progress through the business school curriculum (Aspen Initiative for Social Innovation through Business [AISIB], 2002; Swanson & Orlitzky, 2006).

Other research suggests that business schools do not adequately prepare students in behavioral knowledge and skills but instead emphasize “hard, analytical” coursework (Rynes, Trank, Lawson, & Ilies, 2003). The fact that students can graduate from business schools with a more narrow perspective than they had going in is not lost on the students themselves. Only 22% of MBA students polled said that business schools are doing a lot to prepare them ethically (ISIB, 2002). Not even stockholders are served by this narrow view, as demonstrated by the disastrous financial meltdown following the spate of corporate scandals in the first one half of the present decade. It should come as no surprise to anyone that business education faces a crisis of legitimacy (Adler, 2002; Gates, 2002; Ghoshal, 2003). The problem is not a dearth of technical knowledge. Rather, technical knowledge

has been placed in the service of fraudulent behavior and questionable values (Mitroff, 2004; Waddock, 2003).

Alternatively, some studies have suggested that technical knowledge and value awareness can go hand in hand. For instance, Grob (1995) found that one’s personal-philosophical values have the strongest effect on socially responsible environmental behavior. Similarly, in a multinational investigation, Schultz and Zelezny (1998) found that particular consumer values influenced proenvironmental behaviors. To sum up, personally held values play a critical role in business. Yet because these values are behaviorally subjective, educators tend to exempt them from the kind of scrutiny applied to other aspects of business. As a result, values are rendered invisible or subjected to sloppy reasoning. We redress this state of affairs with a classroom exercise designed to help students surface values and comprehend their role in decision making more concretely. In this article, we describe this laddering exercise, its potential benefits, and the results of our studies pertaining to the efficacy of the exercise.

# The Goal: Helping Students Understand the Role of Values

This article represents a collaborative effort between a marketing professor and two professors of management, our primary goal being to provide students from various subdisciplines of business with a technique for recognizing the role of personally held values in decision making. In other words, we want values, which often remain an invisible force, to become visible to students and the subject of more careful scrutiny.

We decided on a pragmatic learning method whereby students are shown a technique that they themselves can practice. This emphasis on “doing” allows students to get more actively involved in the topic at hand, leveraging the potential for learning (cf. Shively, 1986). Because active learning can shed light on the affective or emotional dimensions that constitute value-driven choices (Bernard & Berney, 1983), this approach is particularly well-suited for identifying personally held beliefs. Moreover, its efficacy is supported by evidence that social and behavioral skills can be learned and improved on through exposure to educational programs that blend theoretical principles and practice (Rest, Narvaez, Bebeau, & Thoma, 1999; Rynes et al., 2003).

Our primary objective of helping students understand the role of personally held values in decision making involves three ancillary objectives.

First, we want students to achieve a better understanding of their own values. By engaging in various aspects of the exercise, students should have the resources to achieve a better knowledge of their own enduring beliefs.

Our second ancillary objective involves helping students develop the ability to respond effectively and efficiently to the demands of the contemporary workplace. In this environment, the ability to think on one’s feet is increasingly called for, given the levels of uncertainty, complexity, and dynamism, which are magnified by the frequency of cross-cultural interactions and mind-boggling changes in technology. More than ever before, managers, employees, and consumers must deal quickly with novel and unprecedented situations. Understanding the values at stake can help identify and perhaps prioritize decision criteria, a clarity that is often called for. Furthermore, the semistructured interview format of the exercise requires students to ask follow-up questions based on respondents’ answers. Because students will be unable to predict respondent answers prior to the interview, this exercise allows students the opportunity to react quickly to unanticipated events.

Our third ancillary objective is to help students understand and respect diverse perspectives and belief systems. Otherwise, they can easily become myopic decision makers who fail to respect viewpoints held by others. According to studies cited earlier (e.g., Gioia, 2002; Kelly, 2002), business education tends to reinforce such myopia, even though a broader mind-set is needed in global environments marked by cross-functional projects that require shared knowledge, effective communication, and sensitivity to different cultural perspectives.

# The Technique: Laddering Interviews

To recap, we sought an active learning technique to help students understand the role of personal values in decision making, achieve a better understanding of their own values, think on their feet, and respect diverse viewpoints. We picked laddering, a technique by which individuals are interviewed in depth about the reasons behind particular choices. These choices can refer to any decision situation. The business and consumer applications are many: product purchases, investment strategies, charitable donations, and career decisions, to name a few.

The laddering interview technique is based on the means–end concept, which was originally designed to offer marketing managers a way to position products by associating means (physical aspects of products) with ends (consumer values) through advertising. The term ladder itself refers to a set of personally relevant factors across three associated levels, configured from top to bottom as: (a) attributes or features of some entity, (b) consequences
---
# Trocchia et al. / THE LADDERING INTERVIEW

or benefits associated with choosing that entity, and (c) personally held values or beliefs underlying the choice.

As part of the laddering interview process, the respondent ultimately expresses the dominant values driving his or her product choice (Gutman, 1982, 1988; Reynolds & Gutman, 1988). For instance, after laddering interviews were conducted, a manufacturer could advertise a four-wheel drive vehicle (the means, or physical aspect of the product) based on the potential for an exciting life (the end, or consumer value) that such a drive-train configuration provides to consumers.

Long a staple in the marketing-research field, the laddering interview easily lends itself to an active learning exercise whereby students conduct interviews with an individual of their own choosing. The topic can either be assigned or selected by the student. We needed to provide students with a list of values to refer to while they conducted the interviews. We considered several well-known classifications (cf. England, 1967; Frederick, 1995; Kahle, 1983; Rokeach, 1979; Schwartz 1994). For the purpose at hand, we decided to use values from Rokeach’s (1979) survey because it is a well-researched instrument that comprises a diverse set of values (18) that are easily identifiable to students. The values contained in Rokeach’s inventory are located in the appendix.

Although this exercise is somewhat time-consuming, we feel that the overall student learning experience is well worth the effort. The laddering interview has broad applications for students in their everyday lives. Use of probing questions and active listening learned in practice, and in the actual interviews, ought to help students develop their conversational skills as business leaders and mature adults. Furthermore, discussion of ethical criteria for conducting interviews is beneficial not only for this exercise but also for establishing and reinforcing student knowledge regarding ethical philosophies, such as deontology and teleology. Below we present the laddering interview technique to our students as a six-step classroom exercise.

# THE EXERCISE IN SIX STEPS

# Step 1: Discussing Ethical Criteria for Conducting Interviews (time allotment: 30-45 mins)

We feel that a preliminary discussion of interview standards is imperative because students of business are generally unaccustomed to methods involving interpersonal interactions and the qualitative, subjective data they yield. We inform students that ethical standards must be upheld, particularly because the interviews address a topic of potential sensitivity. We provide students with training on proper treatment of human participants, advising them to treat all interviewees with the utmost dignity and respect,
---
# JOURNAL OF MANAGEMENT EDUCATION / October 2007

upholding confidentiality and refraining from casting judgment. We explain the process by which respondents need to sign informed consent forms (explaining that their participation is voluntary and ensuring confidentiality) before the interviews take place.

# Step 2: Explaining Laddering as an Interviewing Technique (time allotment: about 20 mins)

Students are told that laddering is a one-on-one process involving a hierarchy of questions and answers; that is, one person (the interviewer) asks another (the respondent) a series of increasingly pointed questions meant to reveal the underlying reasons behind a decision. During the initial phases of a laddering interview, the focus is on the product features or attributes that factored into a purchase decision. For example, a consumer might claim four-wheel drive as a feature that influenced his or her decision to buy a certain motor vehicle. Employing the laddering technique, an interviewer would then ask a series of follow-up questions meant to ascertain the consumer’s perception of the benefits of four-wheel drive, such as the ability to operate the vehicle off-road. Subsequently, the interviewer would ask more questions meant to surface the values consistent with the stated benefit. The individual who wants to drive off-road, for instance, may be striving to provide himself or herself with a stimulating environment or with some sense of achievement. To summarize, the interview progresses along the lines of revealing the (a) attributes, (b) perceived benefits, and (c) underlying values of a purchase decision.

# Step 3: Providing Examples of Laddering Interviews (time allotment: about 20 mins)

At this point, we provide students with copies of Reynolds and Guttman’s (1988) article on laddering, which documents examples of interviews about wine cooler consumption. With these examples in hand, students take turns reading the scripts of interviewer and respondent. After these readings, the classroom discussion focuses on the attributes, benefits, and values identified in the sample interviews. For instance, some attributes of wine coolers are discussed in terms of fancy labeling, carbonation, and low alcohol content. The perceived benefits of these attributes include, respectively, cultivating a sophisticated image, quenching thirst, and avoiding drunkenness. Finally, individuals who consume wine coolers reveal that their choice of such a drink over other beverage types (e.g., beer or hard liquor) is linked to such personal values as achieving social recognition, securing a feeling of accomplishment, and maintaining a good family life.

# Step 4: Practicing Laddering Interviews

(time allotment: about 30 mins)

When students are familiar with the fundamentals of laddering, we ask one student to volunteer as a laddering participant for an in-class demonstration of laddering in action. The instructor then proceeds with the interview, allowing the other students to witness the interview progression in real time and to think for themselves what questions they would ask the respondent if they were conducting the interview. When this mock interview is complete, students are then asked to form dyads and proceed with their choice of interview topics. This last set of mock interviews is meant to enhance the students’ ability to listen to respondents and follow up with appropriate questions, befitting our goal of helping students respond to novel information intelligently and in a timely fashion. Afterward, we select students to informally present to the class the attributes, benefits, and values that surfaced during their practice interviews.

(time allotment: about 15 mins of students’ own time)

# Assessment of Primary Learning Objective: Helping Students Understand the Role of Personally Held Values in Decision Making

Because we had generated specific objectives for this active learning exercise, we needed to develop measures to ascertain whether those objectives had been met. To assess our primary learning objective, (helping students understand the role of personally held values in decision making), two measures (one quantitative and one qualitative) were conducted among undergraduate business students enrolled in four sections of Basic Marketing at a public university in the southeastern United States. Because this class is a core prerequisite for all business students at the participating university, a cross-section of business students was attained.

The quantitative measure was conducted in three stages. First, students completed a survey instrument containing six items about the role of values in decision making. The survey instrument that the students completed was loosely adapted from Swanson and Orlitzky’s (2006) inventory of items. Survey items were subsequently added, modified, and deleted from that list to provide a measure for the primary learning objective. The revised instrument was pilot tested in a marketing research class and revised in accordance with their observations. Survey items were scored on a Likert-type scale, which ranged from 1 being strongly disagree and 7 being strongly agree.

Second, the students completed the 6-step exercise described in the previous section. Third, students completed the same survey instrument that they had filled out prior to performing the 6-step exercise. Because of student absences during different phases of the study, 22 of 151 total students failed to participate in all three of the study’s phases. Hence, 129 Basic Marketing students were included in the current sample.

Students responded to six items pertaining to the role of values in decision making. Unfortunately, mean responses for the six items showed no significant difference between first and second survey administrations at the .05 level. (See Items 2, 4, 5, 7, 8, and 9 in Table 4.)

# Assessment of Ancillary Learning Objective 1: Helping Students Achieve a Better Understanding of Their Own Values

Accompanying the six items discussed in the quantitative measure of our primary learning objective (discussed above) were three items pertaining to students’ knowledge of their own personal values. Of the three items pertaining to students’ understanding of their own values, however, two demonstrated significant, positive changes between first and second survey administrations, indicating greater awareness of their own personal values. (See Items 1, 3, and 6 in Table 4.) Those two items were “I have a better grasp of what my own personal values are than most people do” (preexercise M = 4.97, postexercise M = 5.39, p < .003) and “I have a good idea of what my own values are” (preexercise M = 5.56, postexercise M = 6.02, p < .001). The third item pertaining to awareness of their own values, “I’ve figured out what I value in life,” showed movement in the intended direction between first and second administrations but was not statistically significantly different (preexercise M = 5.73, postexercise M = 5.88, p < .288). These results provide evidence that participating students may have, in fact, experienced a greater understanding of their own values after participating in the exercise.

It should be noted that demand artifacts may have potentially influenced the results of the current study; that is, students may have been aware that the pre- and postsurveys were conducted to demonstrate some sort of learning on their part. There was no manipulation check included on the second survey to assess student guessing of the study’s goals.

# Assessment of Ancillary Learning Objective 2: Helping Students Think on Their Feet

In a follow-up to the studies conducted to assess the two previously discussed learning objectives, 35 undergraduate students in a Basic Marketing class at the same southeastern U.S. public university as above participated in a subsequent laddering interview exercise. To make the task less time-consuming, Step 5, generation of interview questions, was switched from the classroom to a “students’ own time” activity and the second set of practice laddering interviews were eliminated.

To measure the second ancillary objective, students were asked the following open-ended questions after completing the assignment, “Did this exercise help you ‘think on your feet?’ (In other words, did it help you to think and react effectively in a spontaneously fashion?) If so, how?”

Student responses to this question were overwhelmingly affirmative. Many stated or inferred that they had never conducted an interview before.

# Assessment of Ancillary Learning Objective 3: Helping Students Respect Diverse Belief Systems

In a written, open-ended survey, using the same set of participants as described in ancillary learning objective 2, students were asked the following question: “Did this exercise allow you to respect diverse viewpoints? If so, how?”

Students responded in a convincing fashion that the exercise had in fact aided them in terms of understanding others’ viewpoints. It appears that, because respondents were permitted to express their points of view in an unrestricted forum, their perspectives were able to be heard and clearly understood, perhaps for the first time, by the student interviewers. # Limitations and Conclusion

We described a method by which students can learn about values by participating in a classroom exercise. Although our measures provide a fair degree of support for the effectiveness of this exercise, we recognize that it can be rather time-consuming, especially because students need time to practice interviewing. Moreover, because the technique is somewhat novel, instructors must be prepared for a steady stream of student questions. For these reasons, we suggest small class sizes. Furthermore, we strongly recommend that instructors acquire a working knowledge of value inventories and the interpretive methodology on which laddering is based.

Instructors who wish to maximize student learning with this exercise may wish to incorporate a supplementary reflective assignment. After conducting the interviews, students may be asked to compare, in writing, their values with those of their respondents. They may be asked specific thought-provoking questions such as, “Which values are most important to you?” and “If you were shopping for an item in the same product category, how would your values have influenced the purchase decision?” Going through this process should help students to better understand what their own values are and how their values influence their decision-making behavior.

We believe that this exercise is quite versatile. Although the exercise described here was employed in a basic marketing class, it would be appropriate to use in a wide variety of classroom settings. Professors could well use any number of various values inventories (e.g., Kahle, 1983; Schwartz, 1994), depending on their individual goals and preferences. Furthermore, choices for student interview topics are virtually limitless. Such potential modifications to the exercise described in this article demonstrate the flexibility of laddering as a learning vehicle.

We believe that the technique described here helps students understand the role that values play in decision making. Students who understand their own values and those held by others may find themselves at a distinct advantage when engaging in such workplace activities as negotiating, instructing, predicting customer needs, and resolving conflicts. Furthermore, the technique may also help students think in the moment with self-awareness and to grasp the diverse beliefs held by others. We have laid out a technique that business educators can use to facilitate such learning experiences.
